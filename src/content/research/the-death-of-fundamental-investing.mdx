## The Algorithmic Modernization of the Markets

Over 80% of US volume is algorithmic. To trade against machines, you need to think like one.

> In the short run, the market is a voting machine but in the long run, it is a weighing machine. - Benjamin Graham

The assumption was that "gravity" exists. If a stock’s price deviated from its discounted cash flows, rational agents would arbitrage the difference until price converged with intrinsic value.
While Graham was right, the "voting machine" is now run by HFT algorithms executing gamma-scalping strategies.

The assumption was that "gravity" exists. If a stock’s price deviated from its discounted cash flows, rational agents would arbitrage the difference until price converged with intrinsic value.

But in 2025, that gravity thesis is malfunctioning.

We are witnessing a structural decoupling of price from value. This is not merely a cyclical anomaly, and is more of a result of the an inelastic market, where passive indexing, dealer gamma hedging, and algorithmic liquidity often supersede balance sheets.

## The "Good Company" Fallacy

A common analytical error among retail and traditional institutional investors is conflating business quality with investment quality. Historical data indicates that these two factors are often negatively correlated due to the mechanics of valuation.

The data is clear: buying "great companies" simply based on fundamentals at any price is a losing strategy.

---

## 1. The Growth Trap (The Siegel Paradox)

A common critique of modern market structure analysis is that it ignores the compounding power of great businesses. However, history proves that innovation and growth are worthless to the investor if the valuation is wrong.

Wharton Professor Jeremy Siegel's analysis of the "Nifty Fifty" led to a counter-intuitive conclusion known as the **Growth Trap**.

**Case Study: IBM vs. Standard Oil (1950–2003)**

- **The Business Narative:** IBM dominated the computing era with 12.19% annualized revenue growth. Standard Oil (now Exxon) was in a shrinking sector relative to the total market, with 8.04% growth.
- **The Stock Performance:** Standard Oil (Exxon) outperformed IBM in total shareholder return over the 53 year period.

**The Fundamentals (1950–2003):**   

- IBM Revenue Growth: 12.19% annualized.

- Standard Oil Revenue Growth: 8.04% annualized.

Sector Reality: Tech grew from 3% to 18% of the market; Energy shrank from 20% to 5%.

By every business metric, IBM was the superior company. Yet, Standard Oil outperformed IBM in total shareholder return over those 53 years.

Why? The Valuation Tax. IBM traded at an average P/E of 26.7x, while Standard Oil traded at 12.9x. Standard Oil’s high dividend yield (avg 5.19%) allowed investors to reinvest into more shares at cheap prices, compounding wealth faster than IBM’s pure price appreciation.

## 2. The Microsoft "Dead Money" Era (2000–2016)

We saw this again with Microsoft from 2000 to 2016.

From 2000 to 2016, Microsoft executed brilliantly. They dominated enterprise software, launched Xbox, and grew revenue from ~$23B to ~$85B.

- **Business Performance:** Between 2000 and 2016, Microsoft tripled its revenue and dominated enterprise software.
- **Stock Performance:** The stock returned approximately 0% (excluding dividends) for 16 years.

Microsoft was a "Good Company," but it was a terrible stock because its P/E ratio compressed from ~70x to ~10x. The business had to grow for a decade just to justify the price paid in 2000.

The cause was multiple compression. Microsoft’s P/E ratio contracted from 70x to 10x. The underlying business had to grow for over a decade simply to justify the premium paid in 2000. Fundamentals are a necessary condition for business survival, but valuation is the sufficient condition for investment returns.

This proves that fundamentals may be a necessary condition for business survival, but the structural starting point and valuation is the condition that dictactes the payout of the investment.

## 3. The Impact of Passive Flows and Inelasticity

So, why do valuations detach from reality? This detachment of price from value is partially attributable to the rise of passive capital allocation. Passive equity funds now represent a significant portion of the U.S. market. These vehicles are mandate-driven and price-agnostic. For example, when capital flows into an S&P 500 ETF, the fund essentially must purchase the underlying assets regardless of valuation. Because the majority of market participants are no longer analyzing value. They are blindly allocating flow.

Passive equity funds now account for **over 50% of the U.S. equity market**. These funds are price-agnostic. When capital flows into an S&P 500 ETF, the fund must buy the underlying stocks regardless of whether they trade at 10x or 100x earnings.
Thus, as trillions flow into passive ETFs (SPY, QQQ), the largest "best" companies become liquidity sponges. They are bought indiscriminately, pushing valuations to unsustainable levels.

**The Mechanism:**
When a company is universally recognized as "good," that quality is fully priced in. The passive bid pushes the P/E ratio to the stratosphere.

**The Result:**
You're buying a great asset, but paying a price that assumes infinite perfection.

## The Inelastic Markets Hypothesis (IMH)
A research done by Gabaix and Koijen reveals a startling multiplier effect in modern markets. Because institutional mandates and passive rules prevent capital from moving freely to correct mispricing, the market is surprisingly inelastic.

The Multiplier: Investing $1 in the stock market does not increase the aggregate market cap by $1. It increases it by approximately $5.

In the absence of discretionary sellers at current prices (inelasticity), prices must rise disproportionately to accommodate passive inflows. This implies that market trends are often flow-driven rather than fundamentally derived.

In an inelastic market, there is no seller for every buyer at the current price. To induce a holder to sell to incoming passive flow, prices must rise disproportionately. This means market booms are driven by flows, not strictly by fundamental improvements.

## 4. The Market Plumbing: Gamma and The Feedback Loop

While passive flows dictate the medium-term trend, derivatives markets often govern short-term volatility. Market makers (dealers) who sell options must hedge their directional risk (Delta), creating a feedback loop known as Gamma exposure.

Market makers (dealers) who sell options to retail investors must hedge their risk (Delta). This creates a mechanical feedback loop:

- Positive Gamma Regime (Stabilizing): Dealers are effectively "long volatility." As the market rises, they sell underlying stock to hedge; as it falls, they buy. This suppresses volatility and dampens price movements.

- Negative Gamma Regime (Accelerating): Dealers are "short volatility." As the market falls, they must sell more stock to remain hedged, accelerating the decline. Conversely, they chase rallies.

The Reality: During a "Negative Gamma" event, fundamental support levels (like Book Value) often break instantly because dealer hedging pressure overwhelms value buyers. Understanding whether we are in a positive or negative gamma regime is now a prerequisite for timing entries.

## 5. The Modern Hybrid Approach

The "Death of Fundamental Investing" is a misnomer. It is the death of unconstrained fundamental investing.

The future belongs to the hybrid investor who respects both the fundamentals, and the underlying market structure and dynamics that drives price.

The Machine (Breadth): Algorithms are superior at identifying structural inefficiencies (Gamma profiles, Index Inclusion flows, Factor crowding).

The Human (Depth): Humans are superior at judging idiosyncratic value and unstructured data (management quality, geopolitical risk).

## 6. The Mathematics of Expectation

Markets are a mechanism of discounting the future. If a company is widely agreed to be "good" (e.g., NVIDIA or Costco today), that quality is already priced in.

In quantitative finance, the trade is rarely on the absolute earnings, but on the delta (Δ) between actual results and implied market expectations.
- Priced for Perfection: High P/E stocks imply aggressive growth assumptions. Any deviation results in severe multiple compression (mean reversion).
- Priced for Imperfection: Low P/E stocks imply stagnation or distress. Survival or marginal improvement can result in significant expansion.

High P/E stocks (the "Good Companies") statistically suffer from mean reversion. Low P/E stocks (the "Bad Companies") benefit from it.

## The Verdict: Value is Structural, Not Just Fundamental

The death of fundamental investing isn't about ignoring balance sheets, but more about acknowledging that **Market Structure > Fundamentals**.

Market structure defined by passive flows, liquidity constraints, and algorithmic hedging, unable to be captured by simple fundamental and value methodologies, often dictates the path of returns.

To put it simply, a "cheap" stock can stay cheap forever if the market does not care.

If you buy "Good Companies" without respecting valuation mechanics and algorithmic flows, you aren't investing—**you are just making an investment hoping that the market somehow doesn't recognize the public information you are assessing.**
